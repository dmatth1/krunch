# Customer profile — what we actually compress

## Product focus (narrowed 2026-04-22)

**Krunch targets archives of AI-generated conversational text in
regulated verticals** — specifically healthcare AI scribes, legal AI,
financial AI, and AI customer-support platforms serving those
industries. The data:

- Is text-heavy (natural-language dialogue between users and LLMs,
  plus free-text documents uploaded for context). **Neural wins 15–40%
  over zstd-22 on this shape of data** — validated on enwik8 prose,
  predicted by published LM-compression literature on dialogue.
- Grows fast: the AI chat-log universe is ~30–50 PB/year globally and
  compounding at ~10×/year.
- **Must be retained** under HIPAA (6y), FINRA 4511 / SEC 17a-4 (3–7y),
  attorney-client privilege rules (7y+), SOX (7y). Customers can't
  just delete to avoid our bill.
- Is generated by ~10K companies building on OpenAI/Anthropic APIs —
  too many for the model providers to individually court, too small
  to build their own compression pipelines, big enough to care about
  storage costs.

This is the product's primary wedge. The earlier vertical breakdown
(software logs, fintech records, compliance audit trails, etc.) stays
below as context for how the codec design handles adjacent data
shapes — but we're not pitching those segments first.

## Why "regulated AI chat archives" and not "everything text-like"

The Spike 3 experiment 1 result (2026-04-22) showed our dispatcher
*loses* 2.7% to whole-file zstd-22 on homogeneous repetitive JSON
event streams (GitHub Archive data). That data shape favors zstd's
128 MB sliding-window lookup over neural token-by-token prediction.

Narrowing to chat archives gets us out of that regime:
- Chat content is ~80–90% free-text prose, not structured repetition
- Conversations are heterogeneous enough that the dispatcher's
  per-chunk codec selection pays off
- Per-customer training fits the use case naturally — a healthcare
  scribe's vocabulary differs from a legal firm's, but is
  *internally consistent* per customer

Segments we're explicitly **de-emphasizing** (still supported by the
safety net, but not the sales pitch):
- Structured log streams (HDFS-class, API event firehoses)
- Fintech transaction records (dictionary-friendly structure)
- SIEM / security event pipelines (Datadog / Splunk already own these)

### Estimated storage cost exposure for Harvey

Conservative, bottom-up:
- 9.75M active files × ~50 KB avg legal doc ≈ **~500 GB active corpus**
- Plus multi-TB of raw chat logs (100K lawyers × ~20–50 turns/day × ~8 KB/turn × 250 working days) ≈ **~4–10 TB/year of chat content**
- Plus matter-attached documents from diligence / discovery: 1,300 orgs × ~5 matters/year × 5 GB/matter ≈ **~30 TB/year**
- Total annual net-new storage: **~35–50 TB/year**, growing at 5.5× YoY per the query metric
- At Harvey-scale retention (7+ years, legal hold), accumulated archive **hitting the PB tier by ~2028**

Storage cost (rough, at current cloud prices):
- S3 Standard at $0.023/GB/mo on 100 TB accumulated: **~$28K/year** in direct storage
- HIPAA / SOC 2 / BAA-compliant tiers run 2–4× that → **~$60–120K/year** for the encrypted / audited path a law firm's compliance team would mandate
- Plus compliance-archive vendor markup if they use [Smarsh](https://www.smarsh.com/) / [Global Relay](https://globalrelay.com/) / [Proofpoint Archive](https://www.proofpoint.com/us/products/archiving-and-compliance): multiplier of **10–100×** over raw S3 (per [COMPETITIVE_LANDSCAPE.md](COMPETITIVE_LANDSCAPE.md))

### Krunch savings on Harvey-scale

At a 30% compression win on the free-text portion:
- Direct S3: ~$8–12K/year saved at 100 TB
- HIPAA-tier storage: ~$20–40K/year saved
- Against a compliance-archive vendor: 30% of whatever they're paying (easily 6-figure savings)

The bigger win isn't year 1 — it's that the archive **compounds for
the retention window (7+ years)**. A 30% lower fleet = 30% lower bill
every month for every byte we've ever stored for them. On a Harvey
trajectory that reaches PB scale in 2–3 years, compression savings
become a real line item.

## Why OpenAI/Anthropic don't capture this themselves

Both providers are positioning for chat-archive retention at their
own Enterprise tiers:
- [OpenAI launched the ChatGPT Enterprise Compliance API](https://www.securityweek.com/openai-rolls-out-compliance-api-and-integrations-for-chatgpt-enterprise/)
  — time-stamped interactions + files + workspace config as an audit
  record for FINRA / HIPAA / GDPR regulated customers.
- [Anthropic offers Zero Data Retention (ZDR)](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/zero-data-retention)
  (opt-in) and [custom retention controls for Enterprise](https://privacy.claude.com/en/articles/10440198-configure-custom-data-retention-controls-for-enterprise-plans)
  with minimum 30-day retention.

But **neither sells retention-as-a-service to customers of third-
party LLM-wrapper apps** — only to customers of their own Enterprise
tier. Harvey, Abridge, and every other vertical-AI company is hosted
on OpenAI/Anthropic APIs but is *responsible for its own customer
data retention*. That's our customer.

Risk: if OpenAI or Anthropic expand their Enterprise tier to a
"managed retention" offering for API customers, our market narrows.
Monitoring this is worth its own slot in `COMPETITIVE_LANDSCAPE.md`.

## What HDFS is, and why it's a stress test, not a customer

We ran 10+ training experiments on HDFS NameNode logs in Spike 1 +
Spike 2. On that corpus our neural codec loses to zstd. The HDFS
corpus is:

- **100% machine-generated.** No human content anywhere.
- **>90% template repetition** — the same line shape
  `"YYMMDD HHMMSS PID INFO class: action blk_X src /IP:N dest /IP:N"`
  appears millions of times with only the variable fields changing.
- **Variable fields are near-random IDs** (20-digit block IDs, IPs,
  port numbers) that aren't predictable from context.
- **Ideal for a hash-based sliding-window coder.** `zstd --long=27`
  has a 128 MB window that catches every template repetition
  plus every previous occurrence of a block ID / IP, and encodes
  them as short back-references. That's near the theoretical
  optimum for this data shape.

HDFS is what you'd pick if you wanted to make a neural compressor
look bad. It's a worst-case stress test, not a representative
workload. Most real customer data has human-written content
interleaved with structure, which is exactly where neural
predictors beat window-based coders.

What's NOT on this list: highly templated infrastructure logs like

it's a minority slice of the **compliance/archive** spend. Most of

observability tools (Datadog, Grafana), or dedicated log
management products (Splunk, Loki) — not cheap long-term storage.

The archive use-case customers actually buy storage for tends to be:
- audit trails and compliance records
- legal discovery document stores
- medical / financial records under retention regulations
- support ticket archives
- knowledge-base / documentation archives
- SaaS application logs (which have a lot of free-text content)

All text-heavy. All in the neural-wins column of our data.

## Why customer profile shapes the codec design

The hybrid codec design in `HYBRID_CODEC_DESIGN.md` is specifically
tuned to how this customer profile distributes work:

- **Neural-as-primary** because the majority of bytes in a typical
  customer archive are text-like.
- **Per-chunk codec selection** because real datasets aren't pure —
  they have bursts of templated content (where classical wins)
  interleaved with free-text (where neural wins).
- **Per-dataset zstd trained dictionary** for customers whose data
  is structured (fintech, security events) — pushes the classical
  baseline 2-3× without needing neural.
- **CLP in the menu** so when a customer *does* bring templated
  infrastructure logs, we at least match zstd via a log-specific
  path.
- **Brotli shared-dict** for document archives with many near-
  duplicate versions (legal, docs, wikis) — 90%+ reduction on
  incremental stores of the same content.
- **Safety-net substitution** so we never silently ship a blob
  that's larger than what zstd-dict alone would have produced.

## The customer pitch in one paragraph

"You're a regulated-vertical AI company — you have to keep every
LLM conversation and every customer document for 6–7 years. On your
specific archive, we train a small compression model that beats
generic zstd by 20–40%. You PUT via our API; we store on S3 at
your region; you GET back on demand. On a Harvey-scale customer
that's a six-figure annual line item by year three. On zero
dataset do we store more than zstd alone — the safety net is
built in. Per-customer training means your medical vocabulary
stays separate from our legal customer's contract vocabulary;
nothing cross-contaminates."

## Product-design issues this focus surfaces

Narrowing to regulated-vertical chat archives raises two problems
we need to engineer for (not just claim we've solved):

1. **Per-conversation deletion.** HIPAA right-to-amend, GDPR right-
   to-be-forgotten, and attorney-client privilege releases all
   require removing *specific conversations* from the archive on
   demand. Current blob format is append-only 1 MB chunks; deleting
   one conversation means rewriting its chunk. Either (a) keep
   blobs small enough that rewriting one is cheap, or (b) maintain
   a per-conversation tombstone index so reads skip deleted content.
   Both workable; needs design work before first healthcare customer.

2. **Compliance certification chain.** BAAs, SOC 2 Type II, ISO
   27001, HITRUST — customers in this segment need the *storage
   vendor* (us) to carry these. That's 6–12 months of audit work
   before first enterprise contract. Feasible but not free.

## How earlier spikes refine the product claim

The Spike 1 + 2 HDFS result (neural loses to zstd on templated
infra logs) and the Spike 3 exp 1 result (dispatcher loses 2.7% to
whole-file zstd on structured JSON event streams) together told us
the old "we beat zstd on everything" pitch was too broad. The
refined claim is:

- "We beat zstd by 15–40% on free-text dialogue and document
  archives" — our sweet spot, validated on enwik8 prose at 14%,
  predicted at 30–40% on chat per LMCompress literature.
- "We're never materially worse than zstd on any dataset" — true
  by construction via the Stage 3 safety net (max +1% overhead).
- "We don't lead with templated-log or homogeneous-JSON use cases"
  — those need CLP or whole-file-no-chunking modes the product
  doesn't ship yet, and the win isn't decisive enough to pitch.

The spikes didn't kill the thesis; they narrowed it to a segment
where the product's strengths (per-customer neural + regulated
retention + compliance bundling) all reinforce. That's a better
business than a broad horizontal compression play.
